# Sales Call Prep

## Overview
Use this skill to build a fast, practical prep brief before a sales conversation. The goal is not exhaustive research; it is enough context to run the call with a clear objective, strong questions, and a plan for likely objections.

## When to Use
Use this skill when:
- Preparing for a discovery call, demo, negotiation, proposal review, check-in, or QBR.
- Meeting context is partial and needs to be assembled from notes, CRM history, emails, transcripts, or web research.
- Attendees, company priorities, and open questions need to be translated into a concrete call plan.

Helpful inputs:
- Company or contact name
- Meeting type
- Known attendees and titles
- Prior notes, email threads, or meeting context

## Quick Reference

### Context sources
| Source | What to pull |
|---|---|
| Calendar | Meeting title, time, attendees, description, attachments |
| CRM | Account status, contacts, open opportunities, recent activities, notes |
| Email | Active threads, open questions, commitments, shared materials |
| Chat | Internal context, colleague insights, competitive notes |
| Transcripts | Prior call topics, objections, stakeholder concerns |
| Web research | Recent news, funding, leadership changes, industry context |

### Prep brief outputs
- Meeting objective and success criteria
- Account snapshot
- Attendee notes and talking points
- Context and history summary
- Suggested agenda
- Discovery questions
- Likely objections and response angles
- Clear next-step proposal

## Workflow
1. Gather the known meeting details.
2. Pull account and conversation history from available systems.
3. Add recent external research on the company and attendees.
4. Synthesize a call objective, agenda, and gaps in understanding.
5. Prepare discovery questions that close the most important gaps.
6. Anticipate objections based on meeting type, stage, and history.
7. End with a proposed next step that fits the call goal.

### Research sequence
1. **Meeting facts:** date, time, attendees, meeting type, stated purpose.
2. **Account history:** current status, opportunity stage, recent touchpoints, open action items.
3. **Attendee research:** role, background, likely influence on the deal, useful talking points.
4. **Current events:** company news, hiring, funding, leadership changes, market pressure.
5. **Synthesis:** what matters now, what is still unknown, and what the call must achieve.

## Call Planning Template

### Account snapshot
| Field | Capture |
|---|---|
| Company | Name, industry, size |
| Status | Prospect, active opportunity, customer |
| Current priority | Stated initiative or likely business driver |
| Last touch | Most recent interaction and outcome |
| Open items | Unanswered questions, commitments, blockers |

### Attendee notes
For each attendee capture:
- Name and title
- Likely role in the decision: decision maker, champion, evaluator, blocker, end user
- Relevant background or recent activity
- Last interaction, if any
- One useful talking point or question

### Suggested agenda
1. Open with context from the last interaction or a relevant trigger event.
2. Confirm the purpose of the meeting and desired outcome.
3. Explore priorities, pain points, and constraints.
4. Address known concerns or walk through the relevant material.
5. Align on next steps, owners, and timing.

## Discovery Prompts
Use the smallest set of questions that closes the biggest gaps.

### Current state
- What is the team doing today, and what is working versus failing?
- What changed recently that made this worth prioritizing now?

### Pain and priority
- Which problem is most urgent to solve this quarter?
- What happens if this issue stays unresolved?

### Decision process
- Who else needs to weigh in before a decision is made?
- What does the evaluation and approval process look like?
- What timeline is the team working toward?

### Success criteria
- How will the team judge whether this is successful?
- Which metrics or outcomes matter most internally?

### Commercial context
- Is budget already allocated or still being justified?
- Are other solutions or internal alternatives under consideration?

## Meeting-Type Variations
| Meeting type | Emphasis | Must leave with |
|---|---|---|
| Discovery | Questions, qualification, urgency, stakeholders | Problem definition and next step |
| Demo | Use-case fit, tailored proof, technical concerns | Validation of relevance and evaluation path |
| Negotiation / proposal review | Objections, commercial terms, risk reduction | Path to agreement |
| Check-in / QBR | Outcomes delivered, gaps, expansion signals | Renewal or growth motion |

## Likely Objections
| Objection | Prep response |
|---|---|
| Budget is tight | Tie the conversation to cost of inaction, timing, or phased rollout options |
| Timing is not right | Clarify what would have to change for the project to move forward |
| Need more stakeholders involved | Identify missing stakeholders and secure a concrete follow-up plan |
| Not sure this fits | Prepare role- and use-case-specific proof instead of generic product claims |
| Happy with current solution | Probe for gaps, risk, or new priorities rather than forcing a replacement story |

## Common Mistakes
- Doing broad company research without tying it to the call objective.
- Showing up with questions but no hypothesis about the buyer's situation.
- Treating every attendee as equal instead of mapping influence.
- Overloading the agenda instead of protecting time for discovery.
- Preparing responses to objections without evidence from the account context.
- Ending the call without a concrete next step, owner, and timeline.
