# Marketing Competitive Analysis

## Overview
Use this skill to run structured competitor research for marketing teams. It is built for turning scattered market signals into positioning insights, content opportunities, and one-page battlecards that sales and marketing can actually use.

## When to Use
Use this skill when work involves:
- Building or refreshing a competitor landscape
- Comparing messaging, value propositions, or category framing
- Auditing competitor content strategy and SEO coverage
- Mapping the market by price, capability, audience, or platform depth
- Preparing launch, campaign, sales-enablement, or executive strategy materials
- Updating battlecards after launches, funding, pricing, or product changes

Do not use this skill for:
- Broad market sizing without competitor analysis
- Brand design work detached from market evidence
- Win/loss analysis with no external competitor research

## Workflow
1. Set scope: choose which competitors, segments, and dimensions matter.
2. Gather evidence from direct, third-party, and customer-signal sources.
3. Organize findings by competitor and comparison dimension.
4. Compare messaging, positioning, content, and proof points.
5. Synthesize the implications for strategy, content, and sales.
6. Date-stamp outputs so stale intelligence is easy to spot.

## Research Sources
| Source type | What to pull | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Website and product pages | Headline, CTA, audience, pricing, feature emphasis | Fastest view of positioning |
| Blog, webinars, newsroom | Themes, launch cadence, narrative priorities | Reveals demand-gen strategy |
| Social channels and community discussion | Messaging repetition, sentiment, objections | Shows resonance and backlash |
| Free trial, demo, or onboarding | UX, feature depth, implementation friction | Tests claims against reality |
| Reviews and analyst reports | Repeated praise, complaints, category comparisons | Provides external proof or weakness |
| SEO and search visibility | Ranked terms, content gaps, intent coverage | Surfaces discoverability gaps |
| Job posts and public filings | Hiring bets, investment areas, growth signals | Indicates strategic direction |

### Research discipline
- Capture source URL, date, and what changed.
- Separate observed facts from interpretation.
- Prefer repeated patterns over single anecdotes.
- Treat competitive intelligence as perishable; quarterly deep dives and monthly monitoring are a good baseline.

### Value proposition breakdown
For each competitor, capture:
- Promise: what outcome they say customers get
- Evidence: how they prove it
- Mechanism: how the product delivers it
- Claimed uniqueness: what they say only they can do

### Narrative lens
Check each competitor’s story for:
- Villain: what problem, old way, or risk they attack
- Hero: customer, team, or product
- Transformation: before vs. after state
- Stakes: what happens if buyers do nothing

## Content Gap Analysis

### What to compare
| Area | Questions to answer |
|---|---|
| Topics | Which themes do they own that you ignore, and vice versa? |
| Formats | Do they use webinars, calculators, templates, video, or case studies more effectively? |
| Audience coverage | Which segments or use cases get the most attention? |
| Search demand | Which high-intent terms do they rank for that you do not? |
| Quality | Is content shallow, stale, or unsupported by proof? |

### Opportunity rules
Prioritize gaps that combine:
- High buyer relevance
- Clear competitor traction
- Weak current coverage from your team
- A format your audience actually consumes
- A position you can support with proof, not just opinion

## Positioning Strategy

### Reverse-engineer the positioning statement
Use this frame:

For `[target audience]`, `[company]` is the `[category]` that `[key benefit or differentiator]` because `[reason to believe]`.

This forces each competitor’s strategy into one comparable sentence.

### Build a useful market map
Choose two dimensions buyers actually use to compare vendors, such as:
- Price vs. capability
- Ease of use vs. power
- SMB focus vs. enterprise focus
- Point solution vs. platform
- Innovative vs. established

Then answer:
- Which quadrant is crowded?
- Which quadrant is under-served?
- Where is the team’s strongest defensible position?
- Is the goal to create a category, reframe it, win within it, or own a niche?

## Battlecard Structure
A battlecard should fit on one page and answer what a sales or marketing teammate needs in a live situation.

### Required sections
- Competitor snapshot: what they do, who they serve, pricing model, recent developments
- Their pitch: headline, differentiators, and category claim
- Strengths: where they genuinely compete well
- Weaknesses: repeated complaints, gaps, or implementation pain points
- Our differentiators: 3-5 points with why they matter and what proof supports them
- Objection handling: likely prospect claims and concise responses
- Landmines to set: discovery questions that expose the competitor’s weak spots
- Win/loss themes: who tends to prefer them, and why

### Objection-handling format
| If the prospect says... | Respond with... |
|---|---|
| "They do this too" | Clarify how the approach, depth, or trade-off differs |
| "They are cheaper" | Explain what capability, service, or risk reduction the price difference buys |
| "I hear good things about them" | Acknowledge the real strength, then pivot to the deciding difference |

## Output Checklist
A finished analysis should include:
- Named competitors and scope boundaries
- Source-backed evidence with dates
- A messaging matrix
- A positioning map or equivalent comparison
- 3-5 strategic implications for content, positioning, or enablement
- A battlecard or executive-ready summary for immediate reuse

## Common Mistakes
- Collecting facts without deciding what buying question the analysis should answer
- Overweighting competitor claims without validating them through reviews, demos, or customer signals
- Comparing feature lists while ignoring audience, narrative, and proof
- Treating every gap as an opportunity instead of filtering for relevance and defensibility
- Writing battlecards that attack the competitor instead of helping a teammate handle real objections
- Forgetting dates, which makes old intelligence look current
