# Sales Draft Outreach

## Overview
Use this skill to draft outreach only after research establishes a credible hook. The standard is simple: no generic opener, one relevant value proposition, and one low-friction call to action.

## When to Use
Use this skill when:
- Writing cold outbound email or LinkedIn outreach.
- A prospect, company, or role is known but the message still needs research-backed personalization.
- A trigger event, company initiative, mutual connection, or prior relationship may improve response odds.

Do not use this skill to blast undifferentiated templates across many prospects without research.

## Quick Reference

### Available context sources
| Source | What to pull |
|---|---|
| Web research | Company basics, recent news, hiring, product launches, public content |
| Enrichment | Verified email, phone, background details |
| CRM | Prior relationship, account notes, existing contacts, opportunity history |
| Email connector | Create the draft directly in the mail client |

### Hook priority
1. Trigger event: funding, launch, hiring, leadership change, strategic announcement
2. Mutual connection or shared context
3. Prospect content: post, article, talk, interview
4. Company initiative relevant to the offer
5. Role-based pain point when no stronger signal exists

## Workflow
1. Parse the target: person, company, role, email, or LinkedIn profile.
2. Research first using public sources and connected systems.
3. Confirm the minimum facts before drafting:
   - who the prospect is
   - what the company does
   - why now
   - what personalization hook is real
4. Choose the strongest hook available.
5. Draft one concise message with one value proposition and one CTA.
6. Provide an email version, LinkedIn fallback, and optional follow-up sequence.
7. If an email connector exists, create a draft; otherwise return clean plain text.

## Research Checklist
Before drafting, capture:
- Prospect name, title, and likely responsibilities
- Company description and relevant business context
- Recent news or event that makes outreach timely
- Any relationship history or internal context
- The most relevant proof point or customer story
- The single next action being requested

## Message Structure

### Email framework
- **Subject:** personalized, under 50 characters, no spammy wording
- **Opening:** specific hook that proves research happened
- **Middle:** one problem or opportunity relevant to the prospect
- **Proof:** one short credibility point or similar-company result
- **CTA:** one low-friction ask

### LinkedIn framework
- **Connection request:** under 300 characters, no pitch
- **Follow-up message:** value-first note, soft transition, question instead of hard sell

## Scenario Templates

### Cold outreach
```text
Subject: [their initiative] + [your angle]

Hi [Name],

[Specific hook from research].

[1 sentence on the likely challenge or opportunity].

[Brief proof point].

Worth a 15-minute conversation to see if this is relevant?

[Signature]
```

### Warm outreach
```text
Subject: Following up from [context]

Hi [Name],

[Reference shared context, prior meeting, or mutual connection].

[Why reaching out now].

[Specific value you can offer].

[CTA]
```

### Re-engagement
```text
Subject: [short curiosity subject]

Hi [Name],

[Acknowledge the gap without guilt].

[New reason to reconnect].

[Simple question to reopen the conversation].

[Signature]
```

### Post-event follow-up
```text
Subject: Great meeting you at [event]

Hi [Name],

[Specific memory from the conversation].

[Useful follow-up resource or observation].

[Soft CTA for the next step].
```

## Follow-up Sequence
- **Day 3:** short follow-up with a new angle or clarifying point
- **Day 7:** different value proposition or relevant proof
- **Day 14:** polite break-up message

## Channel Selection
| Condition | Recommended channel |
|---|---|
| Verified email available | Email first, LinkedIn as backup |
| No verified email | LinkedIn connection request, then follow-up |
| Strong prior relationship in CRM | Use that context in the opener before any new pitch |

## Common Mistakes
- Drafting before research is complete.
- Using fake personalization such as naming the company with no real insight.
- Packing multiple value propositions into one message.
- Writing long product descriptions instead of a buyer-relevant outcome.
- Using markdown formatting that will render poorly in email.
- Ending with a vague CTA instead of a specific next step.
