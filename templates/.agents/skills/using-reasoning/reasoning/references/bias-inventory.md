# Bias Inventory

Use this file when the `Psychology` lens or the `Attachment Check` suggests the analysis is being distorted.

Do not dump the whole list into the answer. Pick only the biases that materially change the decision.

## Commitment and Identity Biases

### Confirmation Bias
- **Pattern:** Seeking evidence that supports the current thesis.
- **Signal:** Counterevidence gets explained away instead of incorporated.
- **Countermove:** State the strongest disconfirming case in full.

### Sunk Cost Fallacy
- **Pattern:** Continuing because of past investment.
- **Signal:** Time or money already spent appears as a reason to keep going.
- **Countermove:** Re-decide from today forward only.

### Status Quo Bias
- **Pattern:** Preferring the current state because change feels risky.
- **Signal:** The cost of change is concrete; the cost of staying put is vague.
- **Countermove:** Describe the cost of inaction with equal specificity.

### Ego Defense / Identity Protection
- **Pattern:** Protecting self-image instead of updating beliefs.
- **Signal:** One answer feels morally or personally loaded.
- **Countermove:** Restate the problem in third person.

## Forecasting Biases

### Overconfidence
- **Pattern:** Confidence exceeds evidence.
- **Signal:** Narrow ranges, precise forecasts, low humility about uncertainty.
- **Countermove:** Use outside-view base rates and widen ranges.

### Availability Bias
- **Pattern:** Recent, vivid, or emotional cases dominate judgment.
- **Signal:** A memorable anecdote outweighs boring but relevant data.
- **Countermove:** Ask what typical cases look like, not memorable ones.

### Planning Fallacy
- **Pattern:** Underestimating time, cost, or complexity.
- **Signal:** Best-case timeline presented as likely timeline.
- **Countermove:** Use reference class forecasts, not internal optimism.

## Social and Attribution Biases

### Fundamental Attribution Error
- **Pattern:** Explaining others by character and ourselves by circumstance.
- **Signal:** Another actor is described as lazy, irrational, political, or difficult without structural evidence.
- **Countermove:** Ask what system or incentive would produce the same behavior.

### Mirror Imaging
- **Pattern:** Assuming others value what you value.
- **Signal:** User motives are inferred from your own preferences.
- **Countermove:** Separate direct evidence from projection.

### Authority Bias
- **Pattern:** Giving extra weight to high-status views.
- **Signal:** The case is accepted because a powerful person said it.
- **Countermove:** Strip the claim from the speaker and re-evaluate it.

## Framing and Loss Biases

### Loss Aversion
- **Pattern:** Avoiding a visible loss even when it improves expected value.
- **Signal:** Small sure losses feel worse than larger probabilistic risks.
- **Countermove:** Reframe both options in the same gain/loss language.

### Anchoring
- **Pattern:** Early numbers or narratives distort later judgment.
- **Signal:** First estimate keeps shaping all later adjustments.
- **Countermove:** Generate an estimate from scratch before comparing.

### Framing Effect
- **Pattern:** Equivalent options feel different because of presentation.
- **Signal:** Opinion changes when wording changes but substance does not.
- **Countermove:** Restate the choice in at least two alternative frames.

## Pressure Biases

### Urgency Distortion
- **Pattern:** Time pressure is felt, not verified.
- **Signal:** The decision is treated as irreversible or immediate without proof.
- **Countermove:** Ask what actually happens if the decision waits 24 hours.

### Analysis Theater
- **Pattern:** Complexity is used to signal intelligence.
- **Signal:** The answer becomes more elaborate while less actionable.
- **Countermove:** Force a plain-language restatement and one concrete next step.

## Quick Screening Questions

Use these when you need a fast audit:

1. Which answer do I already want to be true?
2. What evidence would make me change my mind?
3. What cost from the past am I smuggling into a future decision?
4. Which actor's motives am I inferring without direct evidence?
5. What would this look like if I removed the most recent anecdote?
6. Am I using complexity to discover truth or to avoid commitment?
